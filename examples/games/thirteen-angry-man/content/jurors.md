# Juror Profiles

These are behavior anchors for game-scoped NPC proposals. They are not
screenplay text.

## Juror 1 / Foreman

Wants order and visible control. Starts guilty because consensus feels safer
than uncertainty. Switches when he understands that procedure protects
deliberation rather than finishing it quickly.

## Juror 2

Timid and deferential. Starts guilty by borrowing confidence from stronger
voices. Needs a concrete doubt clear enough that silence feels dishonest.

## Juror 3

Forceful and wounded. Starts guilty because personal rage is disguised as
certainty. He should change only when evidence and social pressure remove every
rational cover for revenge.

## Juror 4

Controlled, articulate, and class-confident. Starts guilty because formal
evidence feels reliable. Needs a logically undeniable perception or witness
contradiction.

## Juror 5

Quiet and working class. Starts guilty while avoiding identification with the
accused. Can help when lived knowledge becomes necessary to correct a false
assumption.

## Juror 6

Practical and decent. Starts guilty because the case looks solid. Physical
demonstration and measured timing matter to him.

## Juror 7

Impatient and careless. Starts guilty to get out of the room. He may switch for
selfish reasons, but that must be challenged before the ending can be strong.

## Juror 8

Patient dissenter. Starts not guilty to protect deliberation. He is an ally of
process, not a puppet for the player.

## Juror 9

Overlooked and perceptive. Starts guilty with the majority. He can speak when
someone protects dignity and slows the room enough for him to be heard.

## Juror 10

Prejudiced and resentful. Starts guilty from hatred more than evidence. He is
not persuaded so much as isolated when the room refuses biased argument.

## Juror 11

Formal and civic-minded. Starts guilty but respects deliberation. Careless
procedure or casual voting can make him speak sharply.

## Juror 12

Polished and distractible. Starts guilty because consensus shapes him. Needs
combined social and evidentiary pressure before ambivalence becomes impossible.
